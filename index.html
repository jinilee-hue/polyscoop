Tech Show and Tell

From humanoid robots to AI-powered gadgets — CES 2026 showed the world what tomorrow looks like, through a student reporter's eyes.

JK
Jason Kim
Songpa Campus 2026.01.08
✨ AI Generated
LG Electronics' humanoid robot, unveiled at CES 2026, Las Vegas. Designed to handle household tasks autonomously. (Illustration: AI-generated by Poly Scoop)

Every January, the world's biggest technology companies gather in Las Vegas, Nevada, for the Consumer Electronics Show — better known as CES. This year, CES 2026 ran from January 6 to 9, drawing over 130,000 visitors from more than 150 countries. For the first time, our student reporter was on the ground to see it all up close.

The moment I walked into the main hall, I was surrounded by flashing screens, buzzing drones, and robots — lots of robots. Korean tech giant LG Electronics stole the spotlight early with its new humanoid robot, designed to handle everyday household tasks like folding laundry and carrying groceries. Standing nearly 1.7 meters tall, the robot moved with surprising smoothness, drawing gasps from the crowd around it.

Samsung also made a big impression with its "AI Home" concept — a fully connected home where every appliance, from the refrigerator to the washing machine, communicates through a central AI system. Imagine your fridge ordering milk before you even realize you've run out, or your washing machine texting you when the laundry is done. It sounds like science fiction, but Samsung says it could be in homes within two years.

But it wasn't all about household gadgets. One of the most talked-about exhibits came from a small startup called Vuzix, which showcased a pair of sleek smart glasses that can translate spoken language in real time. You simply wear them like ordinary glasses, and subtitles appear right in front of your eyes — in whatever language you choose. As someone who studies English as a second language, I couldn't help but wonder: would glasses like these make language learning unnecessary? Or would they push us to be even more curious about the world?

AI was everywhere at CES 2026 — woven into nearly every product on display. Healthcare companies showed AI tools that can detect early signs of illness from a simple scan. Education startups presented AI tutors that adapt lessons to each student's pace and learning style. Even music and art were touched by AI, with one company demonstrating a program that composes original music based on your mood.

Of course, not all reactions were positive. In a panel discussion I attended, several experts raised concerns about privacy, job losses, and the growing gap between people who can afford these technologies and those who cannot. One speaker put it plainly: "The question isn't whether AI will change our lives. It already is. The question is whether we will guide that change — or let it guide us."

Walking out of the convention center on the final day, my head was spinning with ideas, questions, and possibilities. CES 2026 showed me that the future isn't something that happens to us — it's something we build together. And as students today, we are the ones who will decide what kind of future that will be.
